Aptamigo

The client is a Chicago startup that aims to make the online apartment search process more efficient. To do this, they need to solve the 2 major obstacles standing in prospective renters’ way: poor listing data and hard to reach leasing offices. We were hired to tackle the latter by building a booking system that will let renters easily schedule tours with the buildings they want.

Core problem

After talking to both renters and leasing offices, we found that core challenge was to balance the conflicting desires of each user group. On one hand, renters want the freedom to book any time they want. With research, we found that it’s because:

  • they want to organize their touring schedule efficientlyThis is especially important for out-of-towners who often only fly in for a weekend to do all of their touring. around their free time.
  • they are used to the instant booking features of consumer apps like OpenTable

On the other hands, Leasing offices want maintain control over tour booking because:

  • As salespeople, they want quality leadsUsually, this means that the renter's budget, move in date and size preference are a good fit with what the building can provide. that are likely to sign a lease.
  • they don’t always know in advance how many agents are in the office for any given dayBecause leasing offices are open seven days a week, employees take days off irregularly..
  • they have a working routine that they don’t want to change without a really compelling reason.

In this scenario, one side’s freedom comes at the expense of the other side’s control. Working with Aptamigo, we tried to find the best way to give each side what they want.

Managing direct oppositions

High lead quality without annoying questionnaires

As a first step, we developed a search and filter system that was strict enough to give leasing offices quality leads, but so strict that renters feel overencumbered with questionnaires.apartmentlist.com is a website that tries to control lead quality by making renters fill a 10 question form upfront. We want to achieve the same goal, without the encumbrance To do that, we first separated the form fields into 3 stages so that it seem less long.

Then, we implemented general form field best practices

Freedom to book tours without messing up agents’ schedule

We needed to give renters the freedom to book tours. Unfortunately, this was impossible as leasing offices flat-out refused all the direct booking concepts we showed them, instead we tried to:

  • Create the illusion of instant booking with a Doodle-style calendar where renters can indicate their availabilities and let agents book within those times.
  • Let renters book tours for multiple buildings on the same page so that it’s easier to coordinate multiple tours in a tight schedule.

Changing expectations

In our interviews, we found that manxy of the problems between the renters and leasing offices were due to a matter of habit and expectation, rather than conflict of interest.

Renter: overcoming simplistic expectations

For example, many renters were frustrated because they were comparing the apartment search process to finding a restaurant on OpenTable. To combat this over-simplistic perception, we displayed tips to help renters understand how each filter is necessary and usefulFor example: specifying a move in date range can help your leasing agent find you a better price, depending on market fluctuations. in the process.

Agents: preserving the routine

On the leasing office side, we found much of the negative reaction to stem from us changing their established work routine. To do this, we integrate as much as possible into their existing tools by discarded our dashboard and calendar concepts in favour of a simple, well-written email.

Email may seem archaic and inefficient on the surface, but the agents we talked to were extremely adept at staying on top of their inbox. By using emails, we played to our users’ competence and reduced development time.

Conclusion: self-serve apartment search

Although there are still many areas where we could improve, the design was deemed by renters and leasing agents to be significantly better than their current workflow. And that was good enough for us to ship a first version. For a next version, I hope to improve the following 2 points.

  1. Increase listing differentiation: many renters expressed that they had trouble narrowing down their choices because they were overwhelmed by the torrent of similar pictures and amenities.
  2. Develop a direct booking system: with more time, I hope that we’ll be able to make deals with leasing offices so that we can integrate directly into their calendar system to provide direct booking.

As for myself, I've learned 2 main lesssons in this project.

  1. Managing opposing desires: Sometimes, conflicts of interests are actually conflicts of expectation. Both sides can get what they want if expectations are addressed. As for real oppositions of interest, both sides can still be balanced with the correct compromises.

  2. Understanding unstated problems Often, what users said was totally different from what they actually believed, either because they couldn’t talk freely in an open office or simply because they were unaware of their own behaviour. To understand their true beliefs and motivations, I’ve learned techniques such as: observing their behaviourThis is especially important when interviewing juniors who can't speak freely within earshot of their managers. and reducing the level of discomfort by having conversations about prototypes instead of people.
